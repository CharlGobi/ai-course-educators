## The magic of a good analogy

Every teacher knows the feeling: you have explained something three times, and half the class still looks puzzled. Then you reach for a different explanation — one that connects the idea to something familiar — and suddenly faces light up.

That moment is an analogy working perfectly.

Good analogies are one of the hardest things to create on demand. AI can generate dozens in seconds — and it can make them South African.

---

## Why analogies matter so much

Abstract concepts become concrete when anchored to familiar experiences. For South African learners, the most powerful analogies draw on:

- Daily life experiences (taxis, markets, load shedding, sport)
- Local geography (townships, farms, mountains, rivers)
- South African history and culture
- Foods, customs, and family structures learners know well

The challenge is that most AI-generated content defaults to American or European examples unless you explicitly redirect it.

## Storytelling as explanation

Stories engage learners in ways that bullet-point explanations cannot. AI can write short explanatory stories — mini narratives that carry a concept through a character's experience.

<PromptBox example="You are a Grade 7 Social Sciences teacher. Write a short story (300–400 words) about a fictional South African character — a 12-year-old girl named Ayasha who lives in Langa, Cape Town — that explains the economic concept of supply and demand. The story should unfold through Ayasha's experience at a market. At the end of the story, add three comprehension questions that connect the story events to the formal economics concepts. Use age-appropriate language for Grade 7." />

## Multiple explanations for one concept

When one explanation does not land, you need another. Ask AI for variety.

<PromptBox example="Explain the concept of photosynthesis in three different ways: (1) using a very simple analogy for Grade 6 learners who struggle with reading, (2) using a step-by-step process explanation for Grade 7 learners at grade level, and (3) using a detailed technical explanation with correct scientific terminology for a Grade 9 class. All three explanations should be relevant to South African plants or crops." />

| History (colonisation) | Comparable power dynamics learners experience today |
| English (sentence structure) | The structure of a praise song (izibongo) |
| Physics (waves) | Sound from a vuvuzela travelling across a stadium |

## Checking your analogies

Not every AI analogy will be culturally accurate or contextually appropriate. Before using an analogy in class, ask:

<Checklist items={[
  "Would my learners recognise the thing being compared to?",
  "Is the comparison culturally respectful and not stereotyping?",
  "Does the analogy actually illuminate the concept, or does it confuse it?",
  "Would this analogy work across the different home-language backgrounds in my class?",
  "Have I verified that the science or factual content in the analogy is correct?"
]} />

<SAExample title="The taxi analogy that worked">
Ms Naidoo was teaching Grade 11 Economics — specifically, the concept of price floors and ceilings. Textbook examples used rent control in New York City. Her learners had no frame of reference.

She asked Gemini: *"Explain price floors using a taxi fare example relevant to South African taxis."*

The analogy that came back used government-set minimum taxi fares — something every learner in the room had experience with. The concept clicked in a way it never had with the textbook example. "I wish I had thought of that myself three years ago," she said.
</SAExample>
