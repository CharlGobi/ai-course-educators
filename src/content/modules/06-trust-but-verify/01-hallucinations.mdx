## AI's biggest weakness

Let us talk about the most important thing to understand about AI tools: **they can be wrong, and they can be wrong with complete confidence**.

This is called a **hallucination**.

Hallucination happens when an AI generates content that sounds plausible, authoritative, and well-written — but is factually incorrect. The AI is not "lying." It is doing what it always does: generating the most likely-sounding response based on patterns it has learned. Sometimes that pattern leads to an accurate response. Sometimes it leads to something that sounds right but is not.

---

## Why AI hallucinates

AI language models like Gemini generate text by predicting what word should come next, based on what they have learned from billions of documents. They do not have a separate "fact-checking" system that verifies each claim before outputting it.

This means that:

- If a topic is well-represented in the training data, AI is generally reliable
- If a topic is obscure, recent, or highly specific, AI may "fill in the gaps" with plausible-sounding but invented content
- If you ask for specific statistics, dates, names, or quotes, AI may generate figures that sound real but are not

---

## Where hallucination is most dangerous for South African teachers

**South African-specific facts:**
DBE statistics, school numbers, specific CAPS requirements, recent education policy changes. Gemini's training data is heavily weighted toward international sources. SA-specific information is less represented and more prone to error.

**Statistics and numbers:**
"What percentage of South African learners pass matric?" — always verify from the DBE's own reports.

**Quotes and citations:**
Never use an AI-generated quote or citation without independently verifying it. AI invents plausible-sounding quotes and references that do not exist.

**Recent events:**
AI has a knowledge cutoff date. Anything that happened recently may not be in its training data.

<SAExample title="A real hallucination example">
A teacher asked Gemini: "What is the matric pass rate in South Africa for 2023?"

Gemini confidently provided a specific percentage. When the teacher checked the official DBE Annual National Report, the number was different.

The lesson: For any statistic you plan to share with learners or parents, **always verify from the official source** — the DBE website, Stats SA, or your school's SGB reports.
</SAExample>

## The verify-before-use rule

Before using any AI-generated content that contains facts, statistics, dates, or specific claims:

1. **Flag the claims** — identify any specific facts that could be verified
2. **Check the source** — use the DBE website, Google Scholar, or your own curriculum documents
3. **Trust your knowledge** — if something sounds wrong to you as a professional, investigate it

This does not mean you need to fact-check every word AI generates. A lesson plan structure or a parent newsletter draft does not need fact-checking. But any content that makes factual claims about the world, South Africa, or your subject matter does.
