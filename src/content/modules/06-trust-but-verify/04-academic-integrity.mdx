## The question every teacher is asking

Learners are already using AI. In many cases, they have been using it longer than their teachers. The question is no longer whether to address AI in your classroom — it is how.

There are two extreme responses that both miss the point:

1. **"Ban it completely"** — Almost impossible to enforce, and ignores a skill learners will need in their working lives
2. **"Ignore it"** — Accepts a situation where AI does learners' work and they learn nothing

The middle path is more interesting: use AI as part of learning, redesign what you assess, and teach learners to use it honestly.

---

## What is actually changing

Traditional assessments assumed that written work reflected a learner's individual thinking and effort. AI changes this assumption significantly.

A Grade 9 learner with access to a phone can ask Gemini to write an essay on any topic in seconds. If the assignment is "write a 500-word essay on the causes of World War 1", AI can produce a plausible-sounding response immediately.

This does not mean essays are worthless. It means we need to think carefully about what we are actually assessing.

---

## Redesigning assessments for the AI age

The goal is to design assessments that require something AI cannot easily fake:

**Local and personal knowledge:**
> *"Describe one way that the theme of justice appears in a book you have read this year. Use a specific scene and explain what it meant to you personally."*

AI does not know which book your learner read or what they personally experienced. Personal, specific responses require the learner to actually engage.

**Process documentation:**
> *"Submit your planning notes and three draft paragraphs alongside your final essay. Your final mark will be based partly on the quality of your drafting process."*

AI can write a final essay, but it is much harder to fake the thinking process.

**Oral assessments:**
> *"You will present your research in 3 minutes and answer two questions from the class."*

AI cannot answer unexpected questions in real time. Oral responses require real understanding.

**Collaborative observation:**
> *"Work with your group to solve this problem. I will observe your discussion and mark you on your reasoning, not just your answer."*

In-class collaborative work is difficult to outsource to AI.

## Creating a classroom AI policy

Having a clear, shared understanding of when AI is and is not acceptable in your class reduces confusion and conflict.

## Teaching learners to use AI honestly

One of the most powerful approaches is to make AI literacy explicit in your teaching.

**Practice prompt writing:**
Have learners write prompts as a classroom activity. Discuss which prompts produce better results and why. This builds the PARTS framework skills they need while making AI use visible and shared.

**Use AI as a thinking partner, not an answer machine:**
Teach learners to use AI for: generating ideas they will then develop themselves, getting feedback on their own draft, explaining concepts they do not understand, and checking their thinking — not replacing it.

**Discuss attribution:**
Ask learners: if AI helped you write this, how should you acknowledge that? This is a genuine digital literacy conversation worth having.

<SAExample title="The honest approach">
Mr Kgomotso teaches Grade 11 Life Orientation in Johannesburg. When AI became a topic his learners were talking about, he addressed it directly in class.

"I told them: I know you have all used Gemini or ChatGPT for homework. I am not going to pretend otherwise. What I care about is that you actually learn. So let's talk about when it helps you learn and when it does your learning for you."

He now includes one AI-assisted activity in every unit, where learners explicitly use AI and then reflect on what it got right, what it got wrong, and what they had to add themselves. Academic integrity is built into the task rather than enforced from outside.
</SAExample>
