## AI has blind spots — and so does all of us

AI systems are not neutral. They are built by people, trained on data created by people, and shaped by the assumptions, histories, and gaps in that data.

The result is that AI can reproduce societal biases — patterns of unfairness that favour some groups and disadvantage others. As a South African educator, this matters deeply: the examples AI reaches for, the images it describes, the cultural assumptions it makes, and the stories it tells can reflect a world that does not look like your classroom.

Understanding bias does not mean you stop using AI. It means you use it with open eyes.

---

## Where bias comes from in AI

AI language models are trained on enormous amounts of text from the internet, books, and other sources. The problem is:

- Most of that text is in English and was written by people in the Global North
- Historical events, cultural norms, and educational systems from Sub-Saharan Africa are underrepresented
- Stereotypes and assumptions in the training data get reproduced in AI outputs
- AI has no lived experience — it cannot understand context the way a South African teacher does

This is not a malicious design choice. It is an unavoidable consequence of who has historically produced most of the world's digitised text.

---

## What bias looks like in practice

Here are examples of bias that might appear in AI-generated teaching materials:

**Cultural assumption bias:** AI suggests a lesson starter about "your family trip to the ski resort" — an experience irrelevant to most South African learners.

**Language bias:** AI writes all examples using Standard South African English, ignoring the richness of multilingual South African classrooms.

**Representation bias:** When asked to describe "a successful scientist", AI descriptions may default to certain demographic assumptions unless you explicitly specify diversity.

**Historical bias:** AI may frame South African history through a Western lens, downplaying African perspectives or agency.

**Gender bias:** AI-generated career examples in some subjects may default to gender stereotypes if you do not challenge them.

## Spotting bias: a practical lens

<TryItNow>
Try this experiment in Gemini:

1. Ask: *"Write a short story about a scientist making a discovery."* Read it carefully. What does the scientist look like? Where are they? What language do they speak?

2. Then ask again: *"Write the same short story, but set it in South Africa, and make the scientist a young woman from a township who is the first in her family to go to university."*

Compare the two stories. Notice how much specificity is required to get content that reflects the South African reality.
</TryItNow>

## A simple bias audit for AI content

Before using AI-generated content in your class, run a quick mental audit:

<Checklist items={[
  "Do the examples and names reflect the diversity of South African society?",
  "Are the contexts familiar to my specific learners, or do they assume a different world?",
  "Does the content reinforce any gender, racial, or cultural stereotypes?",
  "Is African history and knowledge treated with the same respect as European or Western content?",
  "Would a learner from every background in my class feel seen in this material?",
  "Are the professional roles and examples gender-diverse?",
  "Does the language assume a Western or US cultural context?"
]} />

## Countering bias in your prompts

You can significantly reduce bias by being specific in your prompts.

Instead of: *"Write a story about a doctor helping a patient."*

Write: *"Write a story about a female Xhosa-speaking doctor working in a rural clinic in the Eastern Cape. The doctor's name is Dr Mhlongo. The patient is a 70-year-old man who has walked 8km to reach the clinic."*

The more specific you are about South African identity, context, and representation, the more relevant — and less biased — the output will be.

<SAExample title="Bias in a Life Sciences example">
Mr Petersen asked Gemini to generate a worked example about nutrition for Grade 7 Life Sciences. The AI produced an example featuring a balanced meal of salmon, quinoa, and avocado.

"None of my learners eat salmon and quinoa," he said. "That example came from a completely different world."

He added to his prompt: *"Use traditional South African foods — pap, morogo, beans, sweet potato, spinach, chicken. The family in the example lives in a township in Gauteng."*

The second example was immediately relevant and familiar. His learners recognised their own meals. The science landed far more effectively.
</SAExample>

## Teaching learners about AI bias

Being aware of AI bias is a 21st-century digital literacy skill. You can use your own experience of prompting and auditing AI content to teach learners to think critically about technology.

Discussion questions for learners:
- Who wrote the text that AI learned from? What does that mean for whose stories get told?
- Can a computer program be "unfair"? How?
- Why might AI know more about New York than about Soweto?

These conversations build exactly the kind of critical thinking that CAPS Life Orientation and English encourage — and they prepare learners for a world where AI is everywhere.
