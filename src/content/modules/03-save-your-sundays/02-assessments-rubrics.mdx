## Assessments, rubrics, and marking grids

Assessment design is one of the most time-consuming parts of teaching. Creating a fair, well-structured rubric from scratch can take an hour or more — and that is before you write the memorandum.

AI can produce a solid first draft of almost any assessment document in under a minute. Your job is to review it, adjust it for your specific learners and CAPS requirements, and make it yours.

---

## What AI can help you create

- **Rubrics** for written tasks, projects, and oral presentations
- **Memoranda** for tests and examinations
- **Question papers** (structured tests with a mix of question types)
- **Self-assessment checklists** for learners
- **Peer-assessment grids** for collaborative tasks
- **Marking grids** that align with CAPS performance descriptors

<KeyPoint type="tip">
Start with the rubric, then ask AI to generate a sample task that matches it. This saves even more time than generating them separately.
</KeyPoint>

## A rubric in 60 seconds

Here is a prompt that produces a Grade 8 English First Additional Language rubric for a creative writing task.

<PromptBox example="You are an experienced South African CAPS-aligned curriculum specialist. Create a rubric for a Grade 8 English First Additional Language creative writing task. The rubric must align with the CAPS assessment standards for written tasks. Include four criteria: Content and Ideas, Structure and Organisation, Language Use and Vocabulary, and Conventions (grammar and punctuation). Use a 4-point scale: Outstanding (4), Adequate (3), Developing (2), and Beginning (1). Format it as a clear table. The audience is a teacher who will use this to mark 35 learner scripts." />

<TryItNow>
Copy this prompt into Gemini. When the rubric appears, ask yourself:
1. Does each criterion match what I actually want learners to demonstrate?
2. Are the descriptors clear enough that a learner could self-assess using this rubric?
3. Are the performance levels aligned with CAPS terminology?

If anything feels off, type: **"Change [criterion name] to focus on [what you want instead]."**
</TryItNow>

## Generating a test or question paper

AI can draft structured tests that include a mix of question types. Give it clear parameters.

<PromptBox example="You are a Grade 10 Mathematics teacher in South Africa. Create a 50-mark test on Euclidean Geometry for Grade 10. The test must comply with CAPS requirements. Include: Section A — 10 multiple-choice questions (1 mark each), Section B — 5 short-answer questions (2 marks each), Section C — 3 longer proof-based questions (10 marks each). Include a memorandum at the end with model answers and marking allocations. Language level: Grade 10 South African learners whose first language is not English." />

<KeyPoint type="warning">
Always verify factual content, especially dates, formulas, and historical facts. AI can generate plausible-sounding questions with incorrect answers. Check every item in the memorandum before printing.
</KeyPoint>

## Adapting AI assessments for your class

Generated assessments are starting points. Here is a review checklist before you use them:

<Checklist items={[
  "All questions are at the correct cognitive level for the grade",
  "The language is appropriate — not too difficult or too simple",
  "The total marks match your intended test weight in the ATP",
  "The content covered reflects what your class has actually been taught",
  "Questions do not reference unfamiliar contexts or cultural assumptions",
  "The memorandum answers are factually correct (verify each one)",
  "Diagrams or figures required are noted clearly"
]} />

## Self-assessment and peer-assessment tools

Learner self-assessment is powerful for metacognition — but only if the checklist is written in language learners can understand.

<PromptBox example="You are a Life Sciences teacher for Grade 11 in South Africa. Create a learner self-assessment checklist for a poster project on the Human Immune System. The checklist should have 8 criteria in learner-friendly language (Grade 11 reading level). Each criterion should have three options: 'I did this well', 'I partly did this', and 'I still need to work on this'. Include space for learners to write one thing they are proud of and one thing they will improve next time." />

<SAExample title="Less marking, better feedback">
Ms Dlamini, a Grade 9 Life Orientation teacher in Durban, uses AI to create peer-assessment grids for group presentations. She tells her learners: "AI wrote the first draft of this rubric. We discussed it together and changed two criteria as a class. Now it belongs to all of us."

Her learners are more invested in the rubric because they helped shape it. And Ms Dlamini saved three hours of marking prep time.
</SAExample>
