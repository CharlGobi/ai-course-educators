## Communication that used to take all evening

Ask any teacher what takes more time than it should, and parent communication will appear on the list.

Writing a term newsletter. Drafting a permission letter for a school excursion. Sending a sensitive message to a parent about a learner's behaviour. Composing a letter to the principal requesting resources. Each of these requires a different tone, different structure, and careful word choice.

AI handles the first draft. You handle the judgment.

---

## Newsletters in minutes

A well-structured class newsletter reassures parents, sets expectations, and builds community. It also takes time to write from scratch.

<PromptBox example="You are a Grade 7 class teacher at a public school in South Africa. Write a one-page end-of-term newsletter to parents and guardians. Include: a warm welcome paragraph, key highlights from this term's learning across English, Maths, and Natural Sciences, an important date reminder (report collection is 12 December, 14:00–17:00), a note about the stationery list for next year, and a closing message of appreciation. Tone: warm, professional, and inclusive. Write in South African English. Length: approximately 350 words." />

<TryItNow>
Try this prompt with your own subject, term highlights, and dates. After Gemini responds:
- Read through the whole newsletter
- Adjust any facts to match your actual term (AI will fill in plausible but generic content)
- Add one specific learner achievement that you are proud of — this personal touch matters to parents
</TryItNow>

## Sensitive messages and difficult topics

Some communications require great care — a note about a learner's behaviour, a concern about attendance, a message to a parent who is hard to reach. AI can help you find the right words.

<PromptBox example="You are a Grade 4 teacher in South Africa. Write a short, sensitive letter to a parent whose child has been absent for 8 out of 15 school days this term. The tone must be concerned but not accusatory — you want to open a conversation, not make the parent defensive. Include: a greeting, an observation about the absences (state the number), an expression of care for the child's wellbeing, an invitation to meet or call, and your contact details. Keep it under 200 words. Avoid educational jargon." />

<KeyPoint type="important">
Before sending any AI-drafted communication: read it out loud. Does it sound like you? Does it reflect your relationship with this family? Make any changes that feel necessary. Your voice and judgment matter more than the AI draft.
</KeyPoint>

**Permission letter for an excursion:**
> *"Write a permission slip for a Grade 6 school excursion to the Apartheid Museum in Johannesburg on [date]. Include: trip purpose, departure and return times, cost (R150), what to bring, emergency contact information, and a tear-off permission section for the parent to sign and return by [date]."*

**Meeting request to the principal:**
> *"Write a short, professional email requesting a meeting with my principal to discuss concerns about classroom resources in my Grade 8 English class. Keep it respectful and solution-oriented. No more than 150 words."*

**Positive update to a parent:**
> *"Write a brief WhatsApp-friendly message (under 100 words) to a Grade 3 parent celebrating their child's improvement in reading. Make it warm and specific to progress rather than achievement."*

<SAExample title="WhatsApp-friendly communication">
Many South African teachers communicate with parents via WhatsApp rather than formal letters. AI can help here too — just ask it to write in a "WhatsApp-appropriate" style, meaning short, warm, and in plain language.
