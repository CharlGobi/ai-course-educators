## The terms you will use most

Here are the terms from the glossary that come up most often in everyday AI-assisted teaching:

**Prompt**
The message or question you send to an AI tool. The quality of your prompt determines the quality of the AI's response. The PARTS framework (Module 2) is about writing better prompts.

**Hallucination**
When AI generates information that sounds plausible but is factually incorrect. You covered this in Module 6. Always verify key facts before using AI content in class.

**Generative AI**
AI that creates new content — text, images, audio — rather than just analysing existing data. Gemini and NotebookLM are both generative AI tools.

**PARTS Framework**
The prompting framework you learned in Module 2: Persona, Aim, Recipients, Theme, Structure. Your guide to writing prompts that get you what you need.

**Source Grounding**
When AI answers are tied to specific documents you uploaded — like in NotebookLM. Source-grounded responses are more reliable because they quote from real documents.

**Large Language Model (LLM)**
The underlying technology behind tools like Gemini. A very large computer program that has processed billions of words and learned to predict useful responses to questions.

**Bias**
Patterns of unfairness in AI outputs that can reflect the biases present in the data AI was trained on. Covered in Module 6 — always audit AI content for South African relevance and representation.

## Explaining AI to colleagues and parents

Part of being an informed AI user is being able to explain it to others. Here are simple explanations for common questions:

**"What is Gemini?"**
> *"It is a writing assistant made by Google. You type a question or request, and it responds with helpful text. It is like having a very fast and well-read colleague who can write a first draft of anything in seconds."*

**"Is AI safe for schools?"**
> *"The tools themselves are safe if used carefully. The main things to watch are: not entering learners' personal information, always checking AI-generated content before using it, and being transparent with learners about when AI has been used."*

**"Will AI replace teachers?"**
> *"No. AI cannot walk into a classroom, build relationships with learners, read the mood of a class, exercise professional judgment, or be accountable to a community. Teachers do all of those things. AI helps with the administrative and preparation tasks that take time away from those human skills."*

## Staying current as AI evolves

AI changes quickly. New tools, new features, and new terminology appear regularly. A few habits to stay informed:

- **Google for Education blog** — free updates on how AI tools are being used in education
- **Your provincial teacher development centre** — many now offer AI training workshops
- **This glossary** — will be updated as new terms become relevant

You do not need to know everything about AI. You need to know enough to use it confidently, teach with it responsibly, and explain it clearly to learners and parents. This course has given you that foundation.
