## Your permanent prompt collection

The Prompt Library is one of the two most-used parts of this course — the other is the PARTS builder in Module 2. This is where you come back, week after week, whenever you need a ready-made prompt for a teaching task.

Every prompt in the library has been written with South African educators in mind. They use CAPS terminology, reference local contexts, and include `[bracketed]` fields you fill in with your own details.

---

## How to use the Prompt Library

1. Go to the **Prompt Library** page (accessible from the bottom navigation)
2. Filter by **subject**, **grade band**, or **category**
3. Find the prompt you need
4. Click **Copy** — the full prompt is copied to your clipboard
5. Paste it into Gemini or NotebookLM
6. Fill in the `[bracketed]` placeholders with your specific details
7. Hit enter and review the result

The library covers six categories:
- **Lesson Planning** — CAPS-aligned lesson plans, topic introductions, learning objectives
- **Assessment** — Rubrics, test questions, memoranda, self-assessment tools
- **Communication** — Parent newsletters, permission letters, meeting requests
- **Differentiation** — Simplified materials, extension tasks, multilingual support
- **Lesson Enhancement** — Starters, analogies, creative explanations, stories
- **Special Events** — Heritage Day, Youth Day, Mandela Day, cross-curricular projects

## The most-used prompts

Based on the needs identified across South African schools, these are the prompts educators reach for most often:

**Lesson planning:**
> *Generate a CAPS-aligned lesson plan for [subject] Grade [X] on the topic of [topic]. Include: learning objectives, prior knowledge activation, main teaching activities, assessment activity, and homework task. Duration: [X] minutes.*

**Rubric generation:**
> *Create a [X]-criterion rubric for a [task type] for Grade [X] [subject]. Use CAPS performance descriptors: Outstanding (4), Adequate (3), Developing (2), Beginning (1).*

**Parent communication:**
> *Write a [letter/newsletter/WhatsApp message] to parents of Grade [X] learners about [topic]. Tone: warm and professional. Length: [X] words. Include: [key points].*

**Differentiation:**
> *Rewrite the following [material] in three versions: simplified (2 years below grade level), at grade level, and extended (for learners who need a challenge). [Paste original material]*

**Lesson starter:**
> *Generate 3 lesson starter ideas for a [subject] Grade [X] lesson on [topic]. Each should take 3–5 minutes and require no printed materials.*

## Customising prompts for your context

Every prompt in the library is a starting point. The most effective prompts are the ones you have personalised for:

- Your specific subject and grade
- Your school's community and context (urban, rural, township, suburban)
- The home languages of your learners
- Your preferred teaching style and tone

**How to customise:** After copying a prompt, add one or two lines of context before the main instructions. For example:

> *[Context: I teach at a rural school in Limpopo. Most learners speak Northern Sotho as their home language. The school has no computers but learners have basic mobile phones.]*
>
> [Then paste the standard prompt]

This two-line context addition dramatically improves the relevance of AI responses.

## Saving your own prompts

As you develop prompts that work particularly well for your context, save them. The simplest system:

1. Create a Google Doc called "My Prompt Library"
2. Organise it with headings by category (Lesson Planning, Assessment, etc.)
3. Paste in your best prompts with notes about when you use them
4. Share it with colleagues in your department

Over a term, you will build a personal prompt library that is more tailored than any generic collection — including this one.
