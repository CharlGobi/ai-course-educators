## Your new writing partner

Gemini is Google's AI assistant. Think of it as a very capable colleague who can write, brainstorm, summarise, explain, and create content on demand. Unlike a search engine that finds existing information, Gemini **generates new content** based on what you ask.

It is available free of charge at [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com), and there is also a free app for your phone (search "Gemini" in the Google Play Store or App Store).

<KeyPoint title="Free for educators" variant="info">
The standard Gemini app is free to use. Google also offers Gemini for Education through Google Workspace for Education — which gives schools added privacy protections for learner data. Ask your school's administrator about whether your school has this set up.
</KeyPoint>

## What Gemini does best

Gemini shines at tasks that involve **writing, explaining, and creating**. Here is where it will save you the most time as a South African educator:

**Lesson planning**
Ask Gemini to draft a CAPS-aligned lesson plan for any subject and grade. You review, adapt, and make it yours.

**Creating activities and worksheets**
Need a reading comprehension exercise, a crossword, or a matching activity? Gemini can generate these in seconds.

**Writing assessment tasks and rubrics**
Describe what you want to assess and Gemini will draft a task with marking criteria. Always check it against CAPS before using.

**Parent communication**
Newsletters, permission letters, class reminders — Gemini can draft these so you just need to personalise and send.

**Explaining difficult concepts**
Ask Gemini to explain a topic in simpler language, or using a local analogy. Great for differentiation.

**Translating and simplifying text**
Need a handout simplified for learners with reading difficulties? Gemini can help.

## What Gemini is NOT good at

Being honest about limitations is important. Gemini can sometimes be wrong — particularly about:

- **Specific statistics, dates, and facts** (especially recent or local ones)
- **South African-specific information** (like exact CAPS weightings or specific DBE policies)
- **Names and biographical details** (it can "make up" plausible-sounding but incorrect information)
- **Real-time information** (it does not have live internet access in its basic mode)

The golden rule: **use Gemini for drafts, not for facts**. Always verify specific information from official sources like the DBE website or your CAPS documents.

## A quick tour of the Gemini interface

When you open Gemini, you will see a simple chat interface — very similar to WhatsApp or a text message window.

1. **The text box at the bottom** — this is where you type your message or question
2. **The microphone icon** — tap this to speak your prompt instead of typing (useful on mobile!)
3. **The response area** — Gemini's reply appears here
4. **The thumbs up/down buttons** — use these to tell Gemini if a response was useful or not
5. **"Gemini can make mistakes" notice** — a reminder to always check outputs

You can have a back-and-forth conversation with Gemini. If its first response is not quite right, you can type a follow-up: "Make it shorter" or "Add more examples" or "Change the tone to be more friendly." This is called **iterating**, and you will learn more about it in Module 2.

<SAExample title="Your first real teaching prompt">
Here is an example of asking Gemini for something genuinely useful:

*"I am a Grade 6 Social Sciences teacher. Create a short 5-question quiz about South Africa's nine provinces: their capitals, key features, and one famous landmark from each. Format it as multiple choice with four options per question."*

Notice how specific this is. You are telling Gemini: who you are, what you want, who it is for, and what format you need. You will learn this skill step-by-step in Module 2.
</SAExample>

<TryItNow title="Explore Gemini">
Open Gemini and try these two prompts. Notice how differently Gemini responds when you give it more detail.

**Prompt 1 (vague):**
> *"Make a quiz about South Africa."*

**Prompt 2 (specific):**
> *"I am a Grade 6 Social Sciences teacher. Create a 5-question multiple choice quiz about South Africa's provinces and capitals for 11-12 year old learners."*

Which response is more useful? Which would you actually be able to use in class? Keep this question in mind — it is the foundation of Module 2.
