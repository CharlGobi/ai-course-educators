## So what exactly is AI?

Artificial Intelligence, or AI, is software that can learn from large amounts of information and then make decisions or generate new content based on what it has learned.

The simplest way to think about it: **AI is a very clever autocomplete**.

When you type a message on WhatsApp, your phone suggests the next word based on patterns it has learned from millions of messages. Gemini — the AI tool you will use in this course — does the same thing, but on a much larger scale. It has learned from billions of documents, articles, textbooks, and websites. So when you ask it a question, it generates a response by predicting what a useful, relevant answer would look like.

<KeyPoint title="The key word is 'generate'" variant="tip">
Generative AI creates new content based on your request. It does not just search for an existing answer. It writes one. This is why you can ask it to create a lesson plan, and it will write a new one — not copy one from somewhere.
</KeyPoint>

## Three myths about AI — busted

Many teachers feel anxious about AI. That is completely understandable. New technology can feel threatening, especially when news headlines are dramatic. Let us clear up the most common myths.

<LessonImage
  src="/images/modules/01-hello-ai/three-myths.jpeg"
  alt="Three panels busting myths: a robot with an X through it, a simple phone with a checkmark, and a warning sign on a screen"
  caption="Three myths — and the truth behind each one."
/>

**Myth 1: AI will replace teachers.**

This is the biggest fear — and the least likely outcome. AI cannot build a relationship with a child. It does not know that Sipho is struggling at home, or that Amahle learns best when she can draw a diagram, or that your Grade 4 class is exhausted on Friday afternoons. You are the professional. AI is a tool.

**Myth 2: You need to be a tech expert to use AI.**

You do not. If you can type a question into a search engine or send a voice note on WhatsApp, you have the skills to use Gemini. This course will show you exactly how.

**Myth 3: AI is always right.**

This one is important. AI can produce inaccurate or even completely made-up information — this is called a "hallucination." You will learn how to spot and check AI mistakes in Module 6. For now, just remember: **AI is a very capable first draft, not a final answer**.

## AI and the South African classroom

Here is why this matters for you specifically.

South African teachers face real challenges: large classes, limited resources, long hours of marking and preparation, and learners with a wide range of abilities and home languages. AI cannot solve all of these problems. But it can take a significant load off your plate.

According to research, educators who use AI tools report saving **two or more hours per week** on tasks like lesson planning, creating worksheets, writing newsletters, and generating assessments.

Two hours a week is a Sunday afternoon back. It is time to rest, plan, or spend with your family. That is the promise of this course.

<SAExample title="South African example">
Think of AI like having a very capable, very fast, always-available junior colleague who never gets tired. They can draft a lesson plan, write a parent newsletter, create a quiz, or simplify a difficult text — all in seconds. You review it, improve it, and make it yours. You are always the professional in charge.
</SAExample>

<TryItNow title="Your very first AI interaction">
Open **Gemini** in your browser or on your phone. If you do not have it yet, go to [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com). It is free.

Type this message exactly:

> *"Hello. I am a teacher in South Africa. I teach [your subject] to [your grade]. Tell me one interesting fact about [a topic you are currently teaching] that would surprise my learners."*

Read the response. Notice that it is not copying from anywhere — it is generating something specifically for your request. That is AI at work.
</TryItNow>
